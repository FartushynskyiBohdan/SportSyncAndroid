# SportSync: A Mobile-First Dating Prototype for Athletes

If my personal role was different, I should edit the first-person references to reflect that accurately.

## Abstract

SportSync is a niche dating app concept designed for athletes and highly active people. The project explores how a familiar swipe-based matching model can be improved by treating sport identity, training frequency, and shared goals as primary compatibility signals instead of secondary profile details. My individual contribution focused on translating the existing SportSync concept into a mobile-first design approach, supported by route planning, usability analysis, and a high-fidelity prototype plan. Rather than starting from a blank slate, I used the existing web and mobile implementation as a foundation and refined it into a clearer app structure suitable for a Figma navigation demo. The result is a focused prototype that combines dating-app familiarity with community-oriented sports matching.

## 1. Introduction

The main idea behind SportSync is to create a dating app specifically for athletes and active people whose routines, identity, and social lives are strongly shaped by sport. Mainstream dating platforms are successful because they reduce the process of meeting people to a simple and familiar sequence of browsing, matching, and messaging. However, for users with structured training schedules and a strong commitment to sport, compatibility is often influenced by factors that general apps treat as minor details. These include training frequency, sporting discipline, skill level, motivation, time commitment, and willingness to build a relationship around an active lifestyle.

My interest in this area comes from the fact that athlete identity is not only a hobby marker. It often shapes how people spend their time, how they socialise, and what kinds of routines they can realistically share with a partner. A sport-focused matching platform therefore has the potential to feel more relevant than a general dating app because it filters for lifestyle compatibility earlier in the process. Instead of leaving sport as an optional line in a bio, SportSync brings it into onboarding, profile identity, discovery, and match relevance.

This project is also interesting from a design perspective because it sits between two familiar product categories. On one side are dating apps such as Tinder and Bumble, which emphasise fast interaction, high visual clarity, and low-friction matching. On the other side are activity communities such as Strava, which position sport participation as a social identity rather than a background detail. SportSync attempts to combine these ideas into a mobile-first prototype that is easy to understand, visually strong, and specific enough to justify its niche.

For my individual contribution, I focused on the mobile iteration of the concept. I treated the existing SportSync codebase as a practical starting point, then documented how it could be presented more convincingly as a high-fidelity app prototype for the module submission.

## 2. Usability and Background Research

My usability analysis began by looking at three useful reference points: Tinder, Bumble, and Strava. Tinder is relevant because its core interaction model remains one of the clearest examples of low-friction mobile matching. Tinder describes its experience as simple and fun, based around liking, matching, and chatting. From a usability point of view, its main strength is efficiency. The user does not need to learn a complex system before acting. One gesture or one tap leads to a clear result, which supports recognition rather than recall and keeps cognitive load low. This is especially useful on mobile, where sessions are often short and attention is fragmented.

Bumble is relevant because it shows how conversation design can shape the tone of an app. Its current "Opening Moves" feature is explicitly positioned as a way to reduce the pressure of starting a conversation and improve interaction quality. Even though SportSync does not directly copy this feature, the idea is important. It suggests that the app should not only help users reach a match, but also make the transition into conversation feel more comfortable and context-aware. In SportSync, this insight influenced the emphasis on shared sports, goals, and training cues so that users have something meaningful to talk about once they match.

Strava is useful as a contrast because it is not a dating app, but it is a strong example of how sport can function as a social identity layer. Strava describes itself as a community of athletes and frames "social fitness" as a combination of activity and connection. This is important for SportSync because it supports the idea that training habits and sporting identity can be meaningful enough to structure a social platform around. The niche is therefore not arbitrary. It is based on the assumption that users who train seriously may value shared routines, motivation, and pace of life just as much as general attraction.

To make the research more structured, I considered the reference apps using usability principles associated with Jakob Nielsen's heuristics. The most relevant principles for this project were visibility of system status, consistency and standards, recognition rather than recall, and aesthetic and minimalist design. Tinder performs strongly in visibility and recognition because every core action has an immediate and understandable consequence. Bumble performs strongly in conversational framing because it uses prompts and interaction guidance to reduce ambiguity after matching. Strava performs strongly in identity clarity because users immediately understand what the platform is for and what kind of community they are entering.

These observations helped identify the opportunity for SportSync. The gap is not that mainstream apps fail completely, but that they are intentionally broad. As an inference from the reference products, general dating apps optimise for scale, while SportSync can instead optimise for a specific type of compatibility. In practical terms, that means the app should surface sports, experience level, frequency, and relationship goals early in the interaction rather than hiding them inside long text bios.

For market validation, I developed user stories and an example survey instrument rather than fabricating research results. The user stories focus on lifestyle compatibility, willingness to disclose sport-related data, and the need for a fast one-handed mobile interface. The survey questions test whether users would actually value a niche athlete-focused dating experience more than a general platform. This is important because the design must be justified as more than a stylistic variation on Tinder. It needs to solve a clearer problem: helping active people judge compatibility through structured signals that matter to them.

One important insight from this background research is that niche apps must justify their narrower audience through clarity and relevance. If SportSync looks like a generic dating app with sports-themed decoration, it loses its purpose. The prototype therefore needs to make athlete identity visible in onboarding, profile cards, match explanation, and messaging context.

## 3. Design and Development Process

The design and development process for SportSync was strongly informed by the weekly lab structure. In the earlier stages, the project existed primarily as an idea and a page map. The route planning already suggested a substantial system: public pages, authentication, onboarding, discover, matches, messages, profile, settings, safety flows, and admin moderation. This was useful because it revealed that the project was not just a landing page or a single interaction, but a connected application with multiple user states.

From the wireframing and prototyping perspective, the first major design task was deciding which flows truly belonged at mobile top level. The web version contains many routes, but mobile interfaces benefit from a smaller and more stable navigation structure. Material Design guidance for bottom navigation recommends three to five top-level destinations that users need to access directly from anywhere in the app. Based on that, the prototype was reduced to a clearer core: Discover, Matches, Messages, and Profile, with Settings accessed from Profile. This preserves the logic of the existing codebase while improving mobile focus.

The onboarding process was designed as a progressive disclosure flow. Instead of asking for every possible detail on one screen, the user moves through identity, location, sport baseline, discovery preferences, and bio. This is important because the app needs richer data than a general dating platform, but it cannot feel like a long form. Breaking onboarding into structured sections allows the app to gather meaningful matching information without overwhelming the user. It also creates a better sense of progress and supports visibility of system status.

The discover screen became the visual centre of the app. This decision follows the expectations set by mainstream matching apps, but the content inside the card is more specific to the SportSync concept. The user sees the profile image first, but also sees sport chips, distance, training frequency, and relationship goal. This is a deliberate attempt to balance emotional attraction with practical compatibility. The user should be able to make a quick choice, but the choice should be better informed than it would be on a generic swipe interface.

The matches and messages areas were designed to reduce friction after a successful match. One common problem in dating products is that the experience feels strong up to the point of matching, then weaker in conversation. To address this, the SportSync structure keeps matches and messaging as major parts of the app rather than hidden subfeatures. The message layout supports immediate continuation from a selected match, which makes the transition from discovery to conversation feel more coherent.

Profile and settings design were also important because trust is a major part of any dating application. The project includes account management, blocked users, password changes, and delete-account functionality, as well as reporting and moderation routes in the wider system. From a design rationale perspective, these are not peripheral extras. They are part of the app's credibility. A dating app that emphasises community and active lifestyle still needs visible moderation and self-control features if users are expected to trust it.

From a tooling perspective, the project moved across several layers. Figma is the right tool for the high-fidelity submission because it allows fast iteration, consistent components, and a clear navigation demo. The existing web front end in React and Vite was useful for establishing the feature set and route structure. The React Native Expo client was especially important for the mobile iteration because it proved that the concept could already be expressed in native-style screens without rebuilding the backend. The backend API and MySQL schema also influenced the design by making the data model concrete: sports, preferences, likes, matches, messages, blocks, complaints, and moderation are not abstract ideas but real entities within the system.

The navigation map reflects this development logic. Users begin at login or registration, move through onboarding if required, and then arrive at the main app shell. From there they can browse discover cards, inspect fuller profiles, like or pass, view mutual matches, enter conversations, edit their profile, and manage account settings. This architecture supports both breadth and clarity. It is broad enough to demonstrate a serious app concept, but structured enough to be shown in a short Figma demo without confusion.

If I were presenting the prototype to a marker, the main rationale behind each tool and methodology would be simple: use the codebase to ground the project in something real, use Figma to polish the interaction into a high-fidelity academic submission, and use mobile navigation conventions to make the final result feel intentional rather than like a resized website.

## 4. Conclusion

Overall, the project taught me that designing a niche app is not only about choosing a theme. It is about proving that the niche creates a meaningful difference in structure, content, and interaction. In SportSync, that difference is the decision to treat athlete identity as a first-class part of matching rather than an optional extra. This affected the onboarding flow, the discover card, the profile layout, and the way the app communicates compatibility.

If I were to approach the project again, I would begin even earlier with user validation and low-fidelity mobile wireframes before moving into full interface detail. The main weakness of fast iterative development is that some decisions are made for convenience before the mobile information architecture is fully refined. A clear example is that the coded mobile proof of concept initially exposes settings more prominently than the polished prototype really needs. Revisiting navigation hierarchy earlier would have reduced that kind of cleanup later.

The biggest obstacles were balancing depth with simplicity and translating a broad web structure into a realistic mobile app flow. SportSync needs enough data to justify athlete-focused matching, but too much visible information would harm usability. Working through that tension was one of the most useful parts of the process. I also learned that implementation details, such as configuring mobile API access and validating dependencies, matter because a prototype feels more credible when it is connected to a real interaction model.

Given more time, I would expand the project in four directions. First, I would run a real survey and short usability tests with active students to validate the niche assumptions more formally. Second, I would improve the conversation layer by adding prompts or shared-sport icebreakers. Third, I would explore optional integration with fitness communities or event-based matching so that users could connect through upcoming training sessions or local sports events. Finally, I would develop the accessibility side further by testing colour contrast, screen-reader labels, and motion reduction for a broader range of users.

In conclusion, SportSync works well as a mobile app design project because it combines familiar conventions with a clear niche purpose. By adapting an existing concept into a focused mobile-first prototype, I was able to show not only what the app looks like, but why its structure and interaction design are appropriate for the problem it aims to solve.
